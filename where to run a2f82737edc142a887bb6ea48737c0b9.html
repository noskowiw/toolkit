In short:

  • Computations should be performed in regions where carbon intensity is the lowest
  • Performing calculations close to renewable energy projects undertaken by cloud providers increases the chance that the datacenter you’re using

Carbon intensity of the region measures how much carbon dioxide has to be emitted in order to produce 1 kilowatt-hour of electricity. This value varies from hour to hour, but in general it doesn’t deviate much from the regional average.

Performing computations in regions with low average carbon intensity is a good way of ensuring that the process you’re running is responsible for as little emissions as possible.

However electricity consumption of cloud computing server farms can be significant enough that their energy composition may end up being different than that of the region where they’re situated.

Cloud providers do not share specific data on the carbon intensity their datacenters are operating at, so there’s no way of knowing which region is the best to run in at any given time. Some cloud providers however operate their own renewable energy projects. It can be assumed that the data centers proximity to those projects correlates with how much of it’s energy composition they account for. Therefore choosing a region close to one or more of those projects is usually the more sustainable choice.
